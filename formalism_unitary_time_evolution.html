ðŸ”¸ Formalism of Quantum Mechanics: Unitary time evolution#

We begin by formulating our second axiom of quantum mechanics that tells us how a quantum system evolves in time. The formulation builds on the mathematical formalism that we have developed in the previous chapter.

Axiom II (Quantum Dynamics):

The time evolution of a closed quantum system is governed by a unitary transformation. If the system is in state \(|\psi(t_0)\rangle\) at time \(t_0\), its state at a later time \(t\) is given by

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = U(t, t_0)|\psi(t_0)\rangle, \]

where \(U(t, t_0)\) is a unitary operator on \(\mathcal{H}\).

ðŸ”¹ Unitary Time Evolution: Essence and Physical Meaning#

The statement of Axiom II â€” that the time evolution of a closed quantum system is governed by a unitary operator â€” has profound physical and mathematical consequences. We recall that an operator \(U(t, t_0)\) on the Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\) is unitary if:

\[ U(t, t_0)^\dagger U(t, t_0) = U(t, t_0) U(t, t_0)^\dagger = I \]

where \(U^\dagger\) denotes the Hermitian adjoint (conjugate transpose) of \(U\), and \(I\) is the identity operator. The unitary transformations generally:

  • Preserve inner products: $\( \langle \phi(t) | \psi(t) \rangle = \langle \phi(t_0) | \psi(t_0) \rangle \)$

  • Preserve norms: $\( \| |\psi(t)\rangle \| = \| |\psi(t_0)\rangle \| \)$

  • Preserve total probability: $\( \langle \psi(t) | \psi(t) \rangle = 1 \quad \text{if and only if} \quad \langle \psi(t_0) | \psi(t_0) \rangle = 1 \)$

The essence of unitary time evolution is the idea that:

In a closed system, the information contained in the quantum state is neither created nor destroyed as time passes.

  • There is no loss of probability (no decay or dissipation).

  • The evolution is reversible: for every \(U(t, t_0)\), the inverse \(U(t_0, t) = U(t, t_0)^\dagger\) exists.

  • The structure of quantum superpositions is maintained over time.

In other words, for a closed system quantum dynamics is perfectly coherent for all times.

ðŸ”¹ Differential equation for the state vector: SchrÃ¶dinger equation#

Having a unitary time-evolution at our disposal, we can calculate the state vector \(| \psi (t) \rangle\) at any finite time \(t\), given some initial time \(t_0\). This gives us the global time-evolution of the system. If we would like to know how the system changes locally around some time, it would be useful to have a differential equation of motion for the state vector. We can derive such an equation starting from the fundamental postulate by differentiation with respect to time:

\[ | \psi(t) \rangle = {U}(t, t_0) | \psi(t_0) \rangle \]

where \(\hat{U}(t, t_0)\) is the unitary time-evolution operator. Taking the time derivative, we get:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} | \psi(t) \rangle = \frac{d}{dt} {U}(t, t_0) | \psi(t_0) \rangle = \left( \frac{\partial {U}(t, t_0) }{ \partial t} \right) | \psi(t_0) \rangle \]

To express this in terms of the state at time \(t\), we recognize that \(| \psi(t_0) \rangle = {U}^\dagger(t, t_0) | \psi(t) \rangle\), so that:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} | \psi(t) \rangle = \left( \frac{\partial {U}(t, t_0)}{\partial t} {U}^\dagger(t, t_0) \right) | \psi(t) \rangle \]

or equivalently,

\[ \frac{d}{dt} | \psi(t) \rangle = \left( \frac{\partial {U}(t, t_0)}{\partial t} {U}(t_0, t) \right) | \psi(t) \rangle \]

We define:

\[ \Omega(t) := \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)}{\partial t} \, U(t_0,t) \]

Thus, the time derivative of the state becomes:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} |\psi(t)\rangle = \Omega(t) |\psi(t)\rangle \]

We have now an equation of motion for the state vector whose structure is determined by the operator \(\Omega(t)\) whose properties we study in the following section.

ðŸ”¹ Properties of \(\Omega(t)\)#

Since \(U(t,t_0)\) is a unitary operator, it satisfies:

\[ U(t,t_0)^\dagger U(t,t_0) = I \]

Differentiating both sides with respect to \(t\), we obtain:

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( U(t,t_0)^\dagger U(t,t_0) \right) = 0 \]

Applying the product rule:

\[ \left( \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)^\dagger}{\partial t} \right) U(t,t_0) + U(t,t_0)^\dagger \left( \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)}{\partial t} \right) = 0 \]

Now, recall that:

\[ \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)^\dagger}{\partial t} = \left( \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)}{\partial t} \right)^\dagger \]

because differentiation commutes with the Hermitian conjugate.
Thus:

\[ \left( \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)}{\partial t} \right)^\dagger U(t,t_0) + U(t,t_0)^\dagger \left( \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)}{\partial t} \right) = 0 \]

Multiply this equation from the right by \(U(t_0,t)\), using \(U(t_0,t) = U(t,t_0)^\dagger\), and obtain:

\[ \left( \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)}{\partial t} U(t_0,t) \right)^\dagger + \left( \frac{\partial U(t,t_0)}{\partial t} U(t_0,t) \right) = 0 \]

Thus:

\[ \boxed{ \Omega(t)^\dagger + \Omega(t) = 0 } \]

That is, \(\Omega(t)\) is an anti-Hermitian operator.

ðŸ”¹ Definition of the Hamiltonian#

Having established that \(\Omega(t)\) is anti-Hermitian, it is natural to define the a Hermitian operator called Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}(t)\) by:

\[ \boxed{ \hat{H}(t) := i\hbar \, \Omega(t) } \]

The factor \(i\hbar\) ensures that \(\hat{H}(t)\) is Hermitian.

Substituting back into the expression for the derivative of the state:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} |\psi(t)\rangle = \Omega(t) |\psi(t)\rangle = \frac{1}{i\hbar} \hat{H}(t) |\psi(t)\rangle \]

thus:

\[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H}(t) |\psi(t)\rangle \]

We have now derived the time-dependent SchrÃ¶dinger equation from the unitary evolution structure.

ðŸ”¹ Unitary Operators and Norm Conservation#

In quantum mechanics, the evolution of a closed system must conserve probabilities. This is mathematically guaranteed if the evolution is governed by a unitary operator.

ðŸ”¸ Definition (Unitary Operator):#

A linear operator \(U\) on a Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\) is called unitary if it preserves the norm of every state vector:

\[ \|U|\psi\rangle\| = \||\psi\rangle\| \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}. \]

Equivalently, \(U\) is unitary if it satisfies:

\[ U^\dagger U = U U^\dagger = \mathbb{I}, \]

where \(U^\dagger\) is the Hermitian adjoint of \(U\), and \(\mathbb{I}\) is the identity operator.

ðŸ”¹ Norm Conservation Implies Unitarity#

Suppose \(U\) is a linear operator satisfying:

\[ \langle U\psi | U\psi \rangle = \langle \psi | \psi \rangle \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}. \]

Then:

\[ \langle \psi | U^\dagger U | \psi \rangle = \langle \psi | \psi \rangle. \]

Subtracting both sides gives:

\[ \langle \psi | (U^\dagger U - \mathbb{I}) | \psi \rangle = 0 \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle. \]

We now prove that this condition implies \(U^\dagger U = \mathbb{I}\).

ðŸ”¹ Lemma: Vanishing Quadratic Form Implies Zero Operator#

Let \(A\) be a Hermitian operator on a complex Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\). Suppose:

\[ \langle \psi | A | \psi \rangle = 0 \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}. \]

Then:

\[ A = 0. \]

ðŸ”¸ Proof:#

We apply the polarization identity in a complex Hilbert space:

\[ \langle \phi | A | \psi \rangle = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{k=0}^{3} i^k \langle \phi + i^k \psi | A | \phi + i^k \psi \rangle. \]

Since each quadratic term on the right-hand side vanishes by assumption, it follows that:

\[ \langle \phi | A | \psi \rangle = 0 \quad \text{for all } |\phi\rangle, |\psi\rangle. \]

Hence, all matrix elements of \(A\) vanish and \(A = 0\).

ðŸ”¹ Conclusion#

Applying the lemma to \(A = U^\dagger U - \mathbb{I}\) (which is Hermitian), we conclude:

\[ U^\dagger U = \mathbb{I}. \]

Similarly, norm preservation of \(U^\dagger\) implies \(U U^\dagger = \mathbb{I}\). Therefore, \(U\) is unitary:

\[ U^\dagger = U^{-1}. \]

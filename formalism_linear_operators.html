ğŸ”¸ Linear maps, operators and functionals#

A natural way to understand any mathematical structure is to study mappings that preserve that structure. This idea is in the core of mathematics and also physics: we gain insight into a structure or a system not only by studying its elements, but by examining its symmetries and transformations that respect the rules of the structure.

In the case of vector spaces, the core structure is linearity: vector addition and scalar multiplication. So the natural class of mappings to study are those that preserve linear sturcture. Such maps must have two propersties: additivity and homogeneity.

In the following we define such maps depending on what the domain and the target space is. Depending on that slightly different terminology is used.

ğŸ”¹ Definition: Linear Map#

Let \( V \) and \( W \) be vector spaces over the same field \(\mathbb{F} \) (typically \( \mathbb{C} \)).

A function\(T: V \to W\) is called a linear map if it satisfies the following two conditions for all \( |\psi\rangle, |\phi\rangle \in V\) and all scalars \(a \in \mathbb{F} \):

  1. Additivity:

\[ T(|\psi\rangle + |\phi\rangle) = T|\psi\rangle + T|\phi\rangle \]

  1. Homogeneity:

\[ T(a |\psi\rangle) = a \cdot T|\psi\rangle \]

ğŸ”¹ Definition: Linear Operator#

A linear operator is a linear map from a vector space to itself.

Let \( V \) be a vector space over \(\mathbb{F}\). A function \( A: V \to V \) is a linear operator if:

  1. Additivity:

\[ A(|\psi\rangle + |\phi\rangle) = A|\psi\rangle + A|\phi\rangle \]

  1. Homogeneity:

\[ A(a |\psi\rangle) = a \cdot A|\psi\rangle \]

ğŸ”¹ Definition: Linear Functional#

Let \(V\) be a vector space over \( \mathbb{F}\). A linear functional is a linear map from \( V\) to the field \(\mathbb{F}\):

\[ f: V \to \mathbb{F} \]

It satisfies the same linearity conditions:

  1. Additivity:

\[ f(|\psi\rangle + |\phi\rangle) = f(|\psi\rangle) + f(|\phi\rangle) \]

  1. Homogeneity:

\[ f(a |\psi\rangle) = a \cdot f(|\psi\rangle) \]

Linear functionals are the elements of the dual space \(V^*\), which we will define next.

In Dirac notation, linear functionals appear as bras as we will see later:

\[ \langle \psi | : V \to \mathbb{C}, \quad |\phi\rangle \mapsto \langle \psi | \phi \rangle \]

ğŸ”¸ The Riesz Representation Theorem (Finite-Dimensional Case)#

Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional complex inner product space with inner product \(\langle \cdot | \cdot \rangle\).

Then for every linear functional \(f \in V^*\) (that is, every linear map \(f: V \to \mathbb{C}\)), there exists a unique vector \(|\phi\rangle \in V\) such that:

\[ f(|\psi\rangle) = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle \in V \]

This is called the Riesz representation of the functional \(f\) by the vector \(|\phi\rangle\).

ğŸ”¹ Proof (Finite-Dimensional Case)#

Let \(\dim V = n\) and let \(\{ |e_i\rangle \}_{i=1}^n\) be an orthonormal basis of \(V\).

Let \(f \in V^*\) be a linear functional. We want to find a vector \(|\phi\rangle \in V\) such that:

\[ f(|\psi\rangle) = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \forall |\psi\rangle \in V \]

Write any \(|\psi\rangle \in V\) as:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n \psi_i |e_i\rangle \]

Since \(f\) is linear:

\[ f(|\psi\rangle) = \sum_{i=1}^n \psi_i f(|e_i\rangle) \]

Now define:

\[ |\phi\rangle := \sum_{i=1}^n f(|e_i\rangle)^* |e_i\rangle \]

Then compute:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \langle \phi | \psi \rangle &= \left\langle \sum_{i=1}^n {f(|e_i\rangle)^* |e_i\rangle} \Big| \sum_{j=1}^n \psi_j |e_j\rangle \right\rangle \\ &= \sum_{i,j=1}^n f(|e_i\rangle)^* \psi_j \langle e_i | e_j \rangle \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n f(|e_i\rangle)^* \psi_i \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n \psi_i f(|e_i\rangle) = f(|\psi\rangle) \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Thus \(f(|\psi\rangle) = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle\) for all \(|\psi\rangle\), and the vector \(|\phi\rangle\) is unique due to the non-degeneracy of the inner product, completing the proof.

ğŸ”¹ Defining Bras Using Riesz#

We now define the dual vector (bra) corresponding to a ket \(|\psi\rangle \in V\) as the unique linear functional:

\[ \langle \psi | : V \to \mathbb{C}, \quad |\phi\rangle \mapsto \langle \psi | \phi \rangle \]

By the Riesz theorem, this bra \(\langle \psi |\) is an element of the dual space \(V^*\).

This defines a bijection:

\[ V \xrightarrow{\text{Riesz}} V^*, \quad |\psi\rangle \mapsto \langle \psi | \]

ğŸ§  Interpretation#

  • The inner product \(\langle \psi | \phi \rangle\) is interpreted as applying the bra \(\langle \psi |\) to the ket \(|\phi\rangle\).

  • The Riesz theorem tells us that every linear functional on \(V\) arises in this way.

  • This justifies Diracâ€™s notation commonly used in physics:

\[ \langle \psi | \phi \rangle = \text{â€œapply the functional } \langle \psi | \text{ to the vector } |\phi\rangle \text{"} \]

ğŸ”¸ Riesz Representation Theorem in Infinite Dimensions#

In the finite-dimensional case, the Riesz Representation Theorem gives a bijective correspondence between a Hilbert space \(V\) and its dual space \(V^*\) via the inner product:

\[ f \in V^* \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \exists! \, |\phi\rangle \in V \text{ such that } f(|\psi\rangle) = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \forall |\psi\rangle \in V \]

This result extends â€” with some technical care â€” to infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces.

ğŸ”¹ Infinite-Dimensional Version (Rieszâ€“FrÃ©chet Theorem)#

Let \(\mathcal{H}\) be a (possibly infinite-dimensional) Hilbert space over \(\mathbb{C}\).

Then for every continuous linear functional \(f : \mathcal{H} \to \mathbb{C}\), there exists a unique vector \(|\phi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}\) such that:

\[ f(|\psi\rangle) = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H} \]

ğŸ§  The crucial difference is the requirement that \(f\) be continuous. In infinite dimensions, not all linear functionals are continuous â€” but the Riesz representation holds exactly for those that are.

This general form justifies identifying the continuous dual \(\mathcal{H}^*\) with \(\mathcal{H}\) itself via the inner product.

ğŸ”¸ From Dual Vectors to Adjoint Operators#

Now that we understand bras as elements of the dual space \(V^*\), and the inner product as a bridge between kets and bras via Riesz, we can move on to defining the adjoint of a linear operator.

ğŸ”¹ Definition: Adjoint Operator#

Let \(A : \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}\) be a linear operator on a Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\).

The adjoint operator \(A^\dagger : \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}\) is the unique operator satisfying:

\[ \langle \phi | A |\psi\rangle = \langle A^\dagger \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle, |\phi\rangle \in \mathcal{H} \]

In words: the action of \(A\) on the ket \(|\psi\rangle\), tested by the bra \(\langle \phi |\), is equivalent to the action of \(A^\dagger\) on the bra.

This relation defines \(A^\dagger\) completely, due to the non-degeneracy of the inner product.

ğŸ”¸ Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness of the Adjoint Operator)#

Let \(A: \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}\) be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\).

Then:

âœ… There exists a unique linear operator \(A^\dagger: \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}\) such that:

\[ \langle \phi | A |\psi\rangle = \langle A^\dagger \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \text{for all } |\phi\rangle, |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}. \]

The operator \(A^\dagger\) is called an adjoint of the operator \(A\).

ğŸ”¹ Proof

This theorem can be proven by using the Riesz theorem.

  • For each fixed \(|\phi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}\), we define:

\[ \Phi_\phi : \mathcal{H} \to \mathbb{C}, \quad \Phi_\phi(|\psi\rangle) := \langle \phi | A |\psi\rangle. \]

  • \(\Phi_\phi\) is linear a linear functional in \(|\psi\rangle\) as can be proven by using the linearity of \(A\) and the linearity of the scalar product in the second theorem.

  • By the Riesz Representation Theorem, there exists a unique vector \(A^\dagger |\phi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}\) such that:

\[ \Phi_\phi(|\psi\rangle) = \langle A^\dagger \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \text{for all } |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}. \]

Thus:

\[ \langle \phi | A |\psi\rangle = \langle A^\dagger \phi | \psi\rangle \]

for all \(|\phi\rangle, |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}\).

The operator \(A^\dagger\) is therefore well-defined and is also uniquely determined.

ğŸ”¹ Self-Adjoint and Hermitian Operators#

An operator \(A\) is called self-adjoint if:

\[ A = A^\dagger. \]

In physics, self-adjoint operators are often also referred to as Hermitian operators. As we already know, in quantum mechanics, observables (measurable quantities like position, momentum, energy) are represented by self-adjoint operators because they have a nice property that their eigenvalues are real. One of the most important mathematical facts about self-adjoing operators is the spectral theorem that we now shortly address.

Spectral Theorem (finite-dimensional version):
If \(A\) is a self-adjoint operator on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\), then:

  • All eigenvalues of \(A\) are real.

  • \(\mathcal{H}\) has an orthonormal basis consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\).

  • \(A\) can be diagonalized as: $\( A = \sum_k \lambda_k |\lambda_k\rangle \langle \lambda_k|, \)\( where \)\lambda_k\( are the eigenvalues and \)|\lambda_k\rangle$ the corresponding orthonormal eigenvectors.

Thus, self-adjoint operators behave analogously to symmetric matrices over \(\mathbb{R}\): they can be fully diagonalized with an orthonormal basis.

ğŸ”¹ Definition: Unitary Operators#

A linear operator \(U: \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}\) is called unitary if:

\[ U^\dagger U = U U^\dagger = I. \]

Equivalently, \(U\) preserves the inner product:

\[ \langle U\phi | U\psi \rangle = \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \quad \text{for all } |\phi\rangle, |\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}. \]

Uunitary operators generalize the idea of rotations to the Hilbert spaces: they shuffle vectors into other vectors without changing their norms or inner products.

ğŸ§  Key Insights#

  • Adjoint operators generalize the idea of taking the conjugate transpose of matrices.

  • Self-adjoint (Hermitian) operators represent observable quantities and have real eigenvalues.

  • The spectral theorem ensures that any Hermitian operator can be understood via its eigenvectors and eigenvalues.

  • Unitary operators represent symmetries and time evolution in quantum mechanics, preserving probabilities and inner products.

Construction of Position and Momentum Observables via Translation Symmetry#

In this section, we introduce the observables corresponding to position and momentum in a semi-rigorous manner, using physical assumptions and basic operator theory. We avoid referring to ‚Äúposition eigenstates,‚Äù which do not exist in the Hilbert space of normalisable wavefunctions.

1. Postulating the Position Operator#

We assume that there exists a self-adjoint operator \(\hat{x}\), called the position operator, with continuous spectrum, defined on a dense domain in the Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H} = L^2(\mathbb{R})\). We consider a normalisable state \(|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}\) such that:

\[ \langle \psi | \hat{x} | \psi \rangle = x_0 \]

This expectation value corresponds to the mean position of a particle in the state \(|\psi\rangle\).

2. Defining the Translation Operator#

We now postulate the existence of a unitary translation operator \(T(a)\) which translates the expectation value of position by a real displacement \(a\):

\[ \langle \psi | T(a)^\dagger \hat{x} T(a) | \psi \rangle = x_0 + a \]

This implies that \(T(a)\) implements a spatial shift. The action of \(T(a)\) on the position operator satisfies the operator identity:

\[ T(a)^\dagger \hat{x} T(a) = \hat{x} + a I \]

This identity can be verified for all states in a dense subset of the Hilbert space and is consistent with translation symmetry in quantum mechanics.

3. Momentum as the Generator of Translations#

By Stone‚Äôs theorem, a strongly continuous one-parameter group of unitary operators \(T(a)\) must be generated by a self-adjoint operator. Therefore, there exists a self-adjoint operator \(\hat{p}\) such that:

\[ T(a) = e^{-i a \hat{p} / \hbar} \]

This defines the momentum operator \(\hat{p}\) as the generator of translations.

4. Deriving the Canonical Commutation Relation#

We now derive the fundamental commutation relation between position and momentum.

Differentiate the identity

\[ T(a)^\dagger \hat{x} T(a) = \hat{x} + a I \]

with respect to \(a\) and evaluate at \(a = 0\):

\[ \frac{d}{da} \left( e^{i a \hat{p}/\hbar} \hat{x} e^{-i a \hat{p}/\hbar} \right)\Big|_{a=0} = \frac{i}{\hbar} [\hat{p}, \hat{x}] = I \]

This yields the canonical commutation relation:

\[ [\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i \hbar \]

5. Representation in Position Space#

Once the commutation relation is established, one can realize the operators concretely in the Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H} = L^2(\mathbb{R})\) as:

\[ \hat{x} \psi(x) = x \psi(x), \quad \hat{p} \psi(x) = -i \hbar \frac{d}{dx} \psi(x) \]

These forms are consistent with the commutation relation and with the interpretation of \(\hat{p}\) as the generator of spatial translations.

Summary#

  • The position operator \(\hat{x}\) is postulated as a self-adjoint operator with continuous spectrum.

  • A unitary translation operator \(T(a)\) is introduced such that it shifts the expectation value of position.

  • The momentum operator \(\hat{p}\) is defined via Stone‚Äôs theorem as the generator of \(T(a)\).

  • The canonical commutation relation \([\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i \hbar\) is derived without invoking ill-defined position eigenstates.

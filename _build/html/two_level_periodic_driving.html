ðŸ”¸ Dynamics of a Two-Level System Driven by a Periodic Perturbation#

We now solve the dynamics of a two-level quantum system driven by a time-periodic external field.

ðŸ”¹ Setup: Time-Dependent Hamiltonian#

Consider a two-level system \(\{ |g\rangle, |e\rangle \}\) with a Hamiltonian:

\[\begin{split} \hat{H}(t) = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \Omega_0 e^{-i\omega t} \\ \Omega_0 e^{i\omega t} & 2\Delta \end{pmatrix}, \end{split}\]

where:

  • \(\Omega_0\) is the coupling strength (Rabi frequency),

  • \(\omega\) is the driving frequency,

  • \(\Delta\) is the detuning between the driving field and the system resonance.

The time-dependent SchrÃ¶dinger equation reads:

\[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H}(t) |\psi(t)\rangle. \]

We seek an analytic solution.

ðŸ”¹ Step 1: Move to a Rotating Frame#

Define a rotating frame transformation:

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{R}(t) |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle \]

with unitary operator:

\[\begin{split} \hat{R}(t) = \exp\left( -i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z \right) = \begin{pmatrix} e^{-i\omega t/2} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i\omega t/2} \end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

Here, \(\sigma_z\) is the Pauli \(Z\) matrix.

âœ…
This transformation removes the fast oscillations associated with the driving frequency \(\omega\).

ðŸ”¹ Step 2: Derive the Effective SchrÃ¶dinger Equation#

Substituting into the SchrÃ¶dinger equation:

[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} \left( \hat{R}(t) |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle \right) = \hat{H}(t) \hat{R}(t) |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle ]

Expanding the derivative:

[ i\hbar \left( \frac{d\hat{R}(t)}{dt} |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle + \hat{R}(t) \frac{d|\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle}{dt} \right) = \hat{H}(t) \hat{R}(t) |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle ]

Multiply from the left by \(\hat{R}^\dagger(t)\):

[ i\hbar \frac{d|\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle}{dt} = \left( \hat{R}^\dagger(t) \hat{H}(t) \hat{R}(t) - i\hbar \hat{R}^\dagger(t) \frac{d\hat{R}(t)}{dt} \right) |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle ]

Thus, the effective Hamiltonian in the rotating frame is:

\[ \hat{H}_{\text{rot}}(t) = \hat{R}^\dagger(t) \hat{H}(t) \hat{R}(t) - i\hbar \hat{R}^\dagger(t) \frac{d\hat{R}(t)}{dt}. \]

ðŸ”¹ Step 3: Simplify the Effective Hamiltonian#

Compute:

  • The derivative:

\[ \frac{d\hat{R}(t)}{dt} = -i\frac{\omega}{2} \sigma_z \hat{R}(t), \]

thus:

\[ \hat{R}^\dagger(t) \frac{d\hat{R}(t)}{dt} = -i\frac{\omega}{2} \sigma_z. \]

  • The transformation of \(\hat{H}(t)\):

Since \(\sigma_z\) commutes with itself and rotates \(\sigma_x\) and \(\sigma_y\), after some algebra you find:

\[\begin{split} \hat{R}^\dagger(t) \hat{H}(t) \hat{R}(t) = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \Omega_0 \\ \Omega_0 & 2\Delta \end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

âœ…
Thus, the effective rotating-frame Hamiltonian becomes:

\[\begin{split} \hat{H}_{\text{rot}} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \left( \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \Omega_0 \\ \Omega_0 & 2\Delta \end{pmatrix} - \omega \sigma_z \right) = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} -\omega & \Omega_0 \\ \Omega_0 & 2\Delta + \omega \end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

ðŸ”¹ Step 4: Apply the Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA)#

In RWA, we assume that the driving frequency \(\omega\) is close to resonance, so that \(\Delta\) is small compared to \(\omega\), and we drop fast-oscillating terms.

This simplifies the detuning to:

\[ \Delta_{\text{eff}} = \Delta + \omega/2, \]

and the effective Hamiltonian becomes:

\[\begin{split} \hat{H}_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \Omega_0 \\ \Omega_0 & 2\Delta \end{pmatrix}, \end{split}\]

where now \(\Delta\) is interpreted as the effective detuning between the drive and the transition.

âœ…
Now the Hamiltonian is time-independent!

ðŸ”¹ Step 5: Solve the Two-Level Dynamics#

The time evolution is governed by:

\[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle = \hat{H}_{\text{eff}} |\tilde{\psi}(t)\rangle \]

with the initial condition transformed to the rotating frame.

You can solve this exactly:

  • The system undergoes Rabi oscillations with generalized frequency:

\[ \Omega_R = \sqrt{\Omega_0^2 + \Delta^2}. \]

  • The probability to be in the excited state at time \(t\) is:

\[ P_e(t) = \frac{\Omega_0^2}{\Omega_R^2} \sin^2\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right). \]

âœ…
This describes how the population oscillates between \(|g\rangle\) and \(|e\rangle\) under the influence of the periodic drive.

ðŸ§  Key Insights#

  • Moving to the rotating frame removes fast oscillations.

  • Applying the Rotating Wave Approximation simplifies the Hamiltonian.

  • Analytic solutions exist and describe Rabi oscillations driven by the external field.

ðŸ”¸ Bloch-Siegert Shift: A Correction Beyond the Rotating Wave Approximation#

When the rotating wave approximation (RWA) breaks down â€” particularly when the coupling strength \(\Omega_0\) is not much smaller than the driving frequency \(\omega\) â€” the resonance frequency of the system is shifted.

This correction is known as the Bloch-Siegert shift.

ðŸ”¹ Setup: Two-Level Hamiltonian with Periodic Driving#

The full Hamiltonian (before applying RWA) for a two-level system is:

\[\begin{split} \hat{H}(t) = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \Omega_0 e^{-i\omega t} + \Omega_0 e^{i\omega t} \\ \Omega_0 e^{i\omega t} + \Omega_0 e^{-i\omega t} & 2\Delta \end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

The off-diagonal terms oscillate at \(\pm\omega\) â€” this is why RWA usually drops the fast terms.

ðŸ”¹ Key Observation#

  • The terms oscillating at \(+2\omega\) (the counter-rotating terms) usually average out if \(\omega\) is large.

  • But if \(\Omega_0\) is not very small compared to \(\omega\), the counter-rotating terms produce a small but systematic effect:
    they shift the effective resonance frequency.

âœ… This shift is what we calculate next.

ðŸ”¹ Step 1: Identify the Fast and Slow Terms#

Expand the driving field:

\[ \Omega_0 e^{-i\omega t} + \Omega_0 e^{i\omega t} = 2\Omega_0 \cos(\omega t). \]

Thus, the Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ \hat{H}(t) = \frac{\hbar}{2} \left( \Omega_0 \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x + \Delta \, \sigma_z \right). \]

ðŸ”¹ Step 2: Go to the Rotating Frame#

We perform a unitary transformation:

\[ \hat{R}(t) = \exp\left( -i\frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z \right). \]

The effective Hamiltonian in the rotating frame becomes:

\[ \hat{H}_{\text{rot}}(t) = \frac{\hbar}{2} \left[ \Delta \sigma_z + \Omega_0 \left( \cos(2\omega t) \sigma_x + \sin(2\omega t) \sigma_y \right) + \Omega_0 \sigma_x \right] - \frac{\hbar \omega}{2} \sigma_z. \]

Simplifying:

\[ \hat{H}_{\text{rot}}(t) = \frac{\hbar}{2} \left[ (\Delta - \omega) \sigma_z + \Omega_0 \sigma_x + \Omega_0 \left( \cos(2\omega t) \sigma_x + \sin(2\omega t) \sigma_y \right) \right]. \]

âœ…
Thus, even in the rotating frame, there are fast oscillating terms at \(2\omega\).

ðŸ”¹ Step 3: Treat Fast Oscillations Perturbatively#

Now:

  • \(\cos(2\omega t)\) and \(\sin(2\omega t)\) oscillate very fast compared to the timescale of \(\Omega_0\).

  • Thus, you can treat the fast terms perturbatively.

The correction to the energy levels due to the fast terms can be estimated via second-order perturbation theory.

Result:

  • The counter-rotating terms cause a shift in the effective energy splitting.

  • This shift in the resonance frequency is called the Bloch-Siegert shift \(\delta \omega_{\text{BS}}\).

Explicitly, the shift is:

\[ \boxed{ \delta \omega_{\text{BS}} = \frac{\Omega_0^2}{4\omega} }. \]

âœ…
Thus, the true resonance occurs not when \(\Delta = 0\), but when:

\[ \Delta_{\text{resonance}} = \delta \omega_{\text{BS}}. \]

ðŸ”¹ Step 4: Physical Interpretation#

  • The Bloch-Siegert shift pushes the effective resonance frequency slightly higher.

  • It becomes important when \(\Omega_0\) is not much smaller than \(\omega\).

âœ…
Thus, RWA fails to predict the exact resonance condition when the drive strength \(\Omega_0\) is not negligible compared to \(\omega\).

ðŸ§  Summary#

  • Counter-rotating terms oscillating at \(2\omega\) cause a small systematic shift in the resonance condition.

  • The Bloch-Siegert shift is:

\[ \delta \omega_{\text{BS}} = \frac{\Omega_0^2}{4\omega}. \]

  • When \(\Omega_0/\omega\) is not very small, this shift must be taken into account.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Parameters
Omega_0 = 0.2        # Rabi frequency (in units where hbar=1)
omega_drive = 1.0    # Driving frequency
Delta = 0.0          # Detuning (initially assume exact resonance)

# Derived quantities
delta_BS = Omega_0**2 / (4 * omega_drive)  # Bloch-Siegert shift

# Time array
t = np.linspace(0, 100, 1000)

# Effective Rabi frequencies
Omega_R_RWA = np.sqrt(Omega_0**2 + Delta**2)                     # RWA
Omega_R_BS = np.sqrt(Omega_0**2 + (Delta + delta_BS)**2)          # Corrected

# Populations
P_e_RWA = (Omega_0**2 / Omega_R_RWA**2) * np.sin(Omega_R_RWA * t / 2)**2
P_e_BS = (Omega_0**2 / Omega_R_BS**2) * np.sin(Omega_R_BS * t / 2)**2

# Plot
plt.figure(figsize=(10,6))
plt.plot(t, P_e_RWA, label="RWA", linestyle='--')
plt.plot(t, P_e_BS, label="Including Bloch-Siegert shift", linestyle='-')
plt.title("Excited State Population vs Time")
plt.xlabel(r"Time $t$")
plt.ylabel(r"Population $P_e(t)$")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()
